The Need for General Purpose Algorithms
The strategy outlined in the univariate case theoretically generalizes to the
multivariate case (x∈R n).
Ideally, we would solve the first order necessary condition:
∇f(x) =0(3)
and then apply the second derivative (Hessian) test to distinguish between critical
points (to be discussed later).
The Challenge:Solving the system of equations in (3) explicitly (in closed form)
is generally intractable, with few exceptions (e.g., quadratic functions in Linear
Regression).
Univariate:Even solvingf ′(x) = 0 can be impossible analytically (e.g.,
transcendental equations likexcos(x) +e x = 0).
Multivariate:In high dimensions (n≫1), algebraic manipulation is rarely
feasible, and systems of non-linear equations are notoriously difficult to solve.

Global vs. Local Minima
Definition: Local Minimum
A pointx ⋆ is a local minimum if there exists a neighborhood around it (some
δ >0) such that:
f(x)≥f(x ⋆) for allxsatisfying∥x−x ⋆∥< δ(6)
Intuition: It is the lowest point in its immediate vicinity.
Definition: Global Minimum
A pointx ⋆ is a global minimum if:
f(x)≥f(x ⋆) for allx∈R n (7)
Intuition: It is the lowest point in the entire domain.

Diagnosis: The ”Zigzagging” Phenomenon
Observation:The performance of gradient descent deteriorates when the
objective function is highly curved in some directions but flat in others
The gradient oscillates (”zigzags”) across the narrow valley
It makes very slow progress along the “flat valley floor”
Statistical Context:This commonly arises in models withmulticollinearity
(highly correlated features), where the Hessian becomes nearly singular (large
condition number)
The Fix: Momentum methods implicitly ”tell the difference” between curved and
flat directions, accumulating velocity to plow through the flat directions while
dampening the oscillations

Physical Intuition: why it is called “Heavy Ball” method

Step 1: Accumulate Squared Gradients We maintain a running sum of the
