Motivation: Why do we need Graph Neural Networks?
Why not just use an MLP?
The Naive Approach: Flatten the adjacency matrixA∈R N×N and feature matrix
X∈R N×F , concatenate them, and feed the giant vector into a standard
Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP).
Why this can fail catastrophically:
1. Size Variance: MLPs require fixed-size input layers. Real-world graphs have
wildly varying numbers of nodesN.
2. Extreme Sparsity:Ais mostly empty. Real networks haveO(N) edges, but
the matrix requiresO(N 2) space. An MLP would waste millions of
parameters multiplying by zeros.
3. Permutation Sensitivity: The ordering of nodes in an adjacency matrix is
completely arbitrary!

Architecture: The Message Passing Framework
How do we build a Graph Neural Network?
We have our graph topologyG= (V,E) and node featuresX.
Our goal: Compute a new, context-aware embeddingh v for every nodev
that captures both its own features and the graph structure around it.
▶ Note: Advanced graphs also have edge features (e.g., chemical bond types),
which we will incorporate later!
How do we compute this? By having nodes ”talk” to each other!
This brings us to the fundamental algorithm of almost all modern GNNs:
Message Passing.

Digression: Spectral vs. Spatial GNNs
Historically, graph convolutions developed from two very different mathematical
viewpoints. It is crucial to understand the distinction:
1. The Spectral Perspective
Origin: Graph Signal Processing.
Mechanism: Uses the Graph
Laplacian and its eigenvectors to
translate signals into the
frequency domain (the Graph
Fourier Transform), applies a
filter, and transforms back.
Limitation: Computationally
expensive (O(N 3)
eigen-decomposition) and strictly
tied to a fixed graph structure
(transductive).
2. The Spatial Perspective
Origin: Local routing / Message
Passing.
Mechanism: Operates directly on
the physical graph topology by
aggregating features from
immediate neighbors.
Advantage: Conceptually intuitive,
highly scalable (via
mini-batching), and easily handles
dynamic or entirely new graphs
(inductive learning).
The 2017 GCN paper actually started in theSpectraldomain. It established that a first-order
approximation of a spectral filter is mathematically equivalent to a simpleSpatialmessage-passing
step! Today, almost all modern GNNs operate spatially.George Michailidis GNN 36 / 52

Architecture 2: GraphSAGE
GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) solves this by introducing an Inductive
framework with Neighborhood Sampling.
Instead of usingallneighbors, GraphSAGE randomly samples a fixed number
of neighbors (e.g., 10-25) for each node.
This breaks the dependency on the full graph, allowing standard mini-batch
training!
Because it learns an aggregationfunction(rather than relying on a static
matrix bA), it can generate embeddings for entirely unseen nodes.

The Weighting Problem of GCN & GraphSAGE
GCN scales messages based strictly on node degree.
GraphSAGE (with Mean aggregation) weights all sampled neighbors equally.

Architecture 3’: Beyond Local Neighborhoods - Graph Transformers
While GAT applies attention to local edges, Graph Transformers (GTs) treat the
entire graph as a fully connected sequence of nodes.
Full Node-to-Node Attention
The Concept: Every node computes an attention score witheveryother node
in the graph, regardless of whether a physical edge exists between them.
Addressing Limitations: Standard Message Passing (and GAT) suffers from

Graph Transformers: Injecting Topology
If a Graph Transformer computes attention between all nodes, it becomes entirely
blind to the original graph structure! We must explicitly inject the topology back
in via encodings.

Text Transformers use 1D sequence positions (1, 2, 3...), but graphs have no
absolute order.

not literal spatial coordinates, but capture smooth variations over the graph.
2. Structural Encodings (Local Topology)

Pair-level (Spatial): Modifying the attention score between nodeiandj

The Expressivity Problem
We have covered GCN, GraphSAGE, GAT, and Graph Transformers. Are these
models powerful enough to distinguish any two different graphs?
No! Both paradigms have fundamental mathematical limits:
1. Standard Message Passing (GCN, GraphSAGE, GAT)
Upper-bounded by the1-Weisfeiler-Lehman (1-WL) Test. They can not
distinguish certain symmetric structures (e.g., a hexagon vs. two triangles).
The Bottleneck: The choice of theAGGREGATEfunction matters deeply.
(e.g., ”Mean” and ”Max” aggregators lose count information; only ”Sum”
reaches the 1-WL limit).
2. Graph Transformers
Global attention bypasses the local message-passing bottleneck, but
introduces a new one: treating the graph as a featureless set.
The Bottleneck: The expressivity of a GT is entirely dictated by its
Positional/Structural Encodings. Even advanced Laplacian encodings fail to
distinguish strongly regular graphs due to eigenvalue symmetries.

The Limits of Deep GNNs: Two Major Bottlenecks
Why can’t we simply stack 100 layers to learn long-range graph interactions?
Deep GNNs suffer from two distinct mathematical constraints:
1. Over-smoothing (The Convergence Problem)
Mechanism: With every layer, nodes repeatedly average or aggregate features
from a wider neighborhood.
Result: The representations of all nodes blur together, eventually converging
to become mathematically indistinguishable.
Analogy: Mixing too many paint colors together until everything turns into a
uniform muddy brown.
2. Over-squashing (The Bottleneck Problem)
Mechanism:In real graphs, the neighborhood size growsexponentiallywith
each hop. Message passing forces this massive amount of data into a single,
fixed-sizevector.
Result: Important signals from distant nodes get ”squashed” and lost as
noise, preventing the model from learning long-range dependencies.
Analogy: Trying to compress a 1,000-page textbook into a single tweet. The
nuanced, distant details are inevitably lost.

Practical Training 1: Deep GNNs & Regularization
If you must build a deeper GNN (e.g., 8+ layers), you need specific architectural
tricks to prevent the network from collapsing via over-smoothing or over-fitting.
1. Residual (Skip) Connections
The Trick: Add the input of the layer directly to its output:
H(l+1) = GNN(H(l)) +H (l).
Why it works: It gives the model a ”gradient highway” and ensures that a
node retains its original, localized features even after many rounds of
neighborhood aggregation. It directly combats over-smoothing.
2. DropEdge (Data Augmentation for Graphs)
The Trick: Similar to standard Dropout, but instead of zeroing out node
features, we randomly remove a percentage ofedgesfrom the adjacency
matrix during each training epoch.
Why it works: It acts as a powerful regularizer that prevents over-fitting.
Crucially, it also slows down over-smoothing by artificially sparsifying the
graph, limiting how fast information spreads.

Practical Training 3: Scalability & Mini-Batching
Standard deep learning loads data in isolated mini-batches. You can not do this
easily with graphs because nodes are connected!
The ”Neighborhood Explosion” Problem
To compute the embedding for Node A in a 3-layer GNN, you need Node A’s
neighbors,theirneighbors, andtheirneighbors. On dense graphs (like social
networks), this quickly requires loading the entire graph into GPU memory.
Solutions for Large Graphs
Neighbor Sampling (GraphSAGE): Instead of taking all neighbors, we
randomly sample a fixed maximum number of neighbors (e.g., 10) per node
at each layer. This caps the memory footprint.
Subgraph Sampling (Cluster-GCN): We use a clustering algorithm to break
the massive graph into smaller, densely connected subgraphs. We then train
on these subgraphs as standard, isolated mini-batches.
